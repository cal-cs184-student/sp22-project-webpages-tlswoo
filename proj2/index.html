Part 1

        To get a point on a Bezier curve, we use de Casteljau’s algorithm. We begin with a set of n points, and using some given t value, we linearly interpolate between n - 1 successive pairs of points to give us n - 1 linearly interpolated values. This is one iteration of the algorithm; we continue this process on our new set of n - 1 points, until we are left with a single point that lies on the Bezier curve between all original n points. For my implementation, I defined a linear interpolation for 2D points and iterated over the set of all control points n - 1 times, linearly interpolating as I went. I was left with a std::vector containing n - 1 intermediate points of the Bezier curve.

Part 2

        The extension of de Casteljau’s algorithm to Bezier surfaces vaguely reminded me of the bilinear interpolation process we did in our Rasterizer project—we linearly interpolate horizontally across our control points, then use a final “vertical” lerp across the resulting values. For Bezier surfaces, we use de Casteljau’s algorithm on each row to get the final point lying on that horizontal Bezier curve, then linearly interpolate across the Bezier curve points of each row to get a single point on our Bezier surface. I began by defining a linear interpolation function for 3D points. I then ran de Casteljau’s algorithm on each row of the control points, giving me n Bezier curve values. Finally, I linearly interpolated across said values to give me the final value on the Bezier surface.

Part 6

        Implementing loop subdivision was the most difficult part of the project for me. I used the method detailed in the project skeleton, by pre-calculating both old and new vertex positions, then using a series of edge splits and flips to create an upscaled mesh. During this process however, I noticed that after one or two seemingly correct iterations of upscaling, the subsequent upscales would have holes or patches of irregularities.

        Upon closer inspection on these irregular areas, I noticed that the vertex that was way off was pointing to a half edge that wasn’t on one of its incident edges. This led me to believe that either my edgeFlip or edgeSplit functions were incorrect; it turned out that both were in fact wrong in some way or another, so I rewrote both of them and finally my loop subdivision was working as intended.

        After subdivision, it seems that sharp corners and edges become more pronounced while flatter areas “smooth out” to some degree. For very sharp corners, this effect is difficult to deal with, but by splitting the edges incident to a particular sharp vertex, the surrounding areas of that mesh “soften up” a bit more and the edge is less pronounced.

        For cube.dae, this issue is quite pronounced after a few levels of subdivision. The edges of the cube remain even in the subdivided mesh, which leaves us with an asymmetrical shape.

To get the cube to subdivide symmetrically, I started by splitting every edge across each face of the cube; thus, every edge has symmetrical edges. I then flipped each edge on the edge of the cube, giving me a star-like, symmetrical shape. After subdivision, although there were some sharp edges on each corner, the result was indeed symmetric.
